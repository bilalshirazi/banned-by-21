# Knowledge Base: Quebec Laicity Laws (Bill 21 & Bill 94)

## 1. Bill 21: An Act respecting the laicity of the State
*   **Introduction & Purpose:** Introduced in 2019 by Simon Jolin-Barrette, Minister of Immigration, Diversity and Inclusiveness, the bill aims to affirm the laicity (secularism) of the State based on the separation of State and religions, religious neutrality, equality of citizens, and freedom of conscience and religion.
*   **Prohibitions on Attire:** The law prohibits specific public sector workers from wearing religious symbols (such as hijabs, kippahs, or turbans) while exercising their functions. 
*   **Affected Professions:** Regulated roles include teachers and principals in public educational institutions, peace officers, government-employed lawyers and notaries, administrative justices of the peace, and the President and Vice-Presidents of the National Assembly.
*   **Face-Covering Mandates:** Personnel members must exercise their functions with their face uncovered. Additionally, citizens receiving services from these personnel must have their face uncovered for identity verification or security reasons, though exemptions exist for health reasons or handicaps.

## 2. Bill 94: An Act to reinforce laicity in the education network
*   **Introduction & Purpose:** Introduced in 2025 by Bernard Drainville, Minister of Education, this bill expands laicity rules specifically within the public school system and private educational institutions.
*   **Expansion of Prohibitions:** Bill 94 expands the ban on wearing religious symbols to all school service centre personnel, including support staff, daycare workers, supervisors, and special education technicians.
*   **Student and Parent Mandates:** Students, homeschooled children, and their parents are legally required to have their faces uncovered when on school premises or when receiving services from a school service centre.
*   **Religious Practices:** The bill expressly prohibits the use of school premises (such as rooms or immovables) for religious practices, including overt prayers. 
*   **Curriculum & Conduct:** Teachers are required to submit instructional planning to principals, who evaluate them annually to ensure compliance with State laicity. The conduct of all staff and school governing boards must be "free of religious considerations".

## 3. Legal and Constitutional Context
*   **Notwithstanding Clause:** Both Bill 21 and Bill 94 invoke the notwithstanding clause to operate despite Sections 2 (fundamental freedoms, including freedom of conscience and religion) and 7 to 15 (equality rights) of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms.
*   **Quebec Charter Amendments:** The legislation amends the Quebec *Charter of human rights and freedoms* to explicitly state that the "Québec nation considers State laicity to be of fundamental importance".
*   **No Religious Accommodations:** Bill 94 strictly prohibits the granting of any accommodation, derogation, or adaptation on religious grounds concerning the prohibition of religious symbols or the mandate to have faces uncovered. 
*   **Note on Exemptions:** While historical "grandfather clauses" existed in early drafts, they are increasingly considered irrelevant in practice due to strict enforcement and the immediate loss of status upon any job change or promotion. This application assumes a hardline interpretation of the laws to better reflect the risks faced by employees.

## 4. Socio-Economic Impacts and Enforcement
*   **Job Losses & Ultimatums:** Following the introduction of Bill 94, school monitors and support staff have been given ultimatums to remove their hijabs or lose their jobs, resulting in firings. 
*   **Staffing Shortages:** The Montreal Association of School Principals warned that hundreds of employees could lose their jobs due to Bill 94, exacerbating existing labour shortages, as over 1,000 support staff positions were already unfilled at the start of the school year.
*   **High-Profile Removals:** In 2021, an elementary school teacher named Fatemeh Anvari was removed from her classroom in Chelsea, Quebec, because she wore a hijab, sparking national outcry.
*   **Impact on Muslim Women:** A study by the National Council of Canadian Muslims (NCCM) reported that 88% of surveyed Muslim women felt Quebec became a less welcoming place to live, 71% considered leaving the province, and over 50% reported an increase in questions about their religious identity during job interviews.

## 5. Advocacy and Opposition
*   **Supreme Court Challenge:** The NCCM, in partnership with the Canadian Civil Liberties Association (CCLA), is actively challenging the legislation and was granted leave to appeal Bill 21 at the Supreme Court of Canada in 2025.
*   **Political Opposition:** Provinces across Canada, including Ontario, Alberta, BC, and Nova Scotia, have spoken out against the legislation. Former Manitoba Premier Brian Pallister publicly invited targeted civil servants to move to Manitoba, stating, "we're not big on clothing police here".
*   **International Criticism:** Three United Nations legal experts sent a letter to the Canadian mission in Geneva warning that the legislation violates fundamental freedoms by disadvantaging or excluding people from public positions.
